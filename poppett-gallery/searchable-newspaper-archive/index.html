26 Apr 1888 – San Francisco Chronicle 6 – RapBUS

“The Index of San Bernardino,” –

The Index gives a chapter in the story, thus: “It isn’t every day in the year that funny things happen. Sometimes they double up, and then again they quadruple. The latest is a trifle amusing. It is thrown out in chunks, not in the plain, everyday way. The reader possessed with a memory will recall the article published in the Index reciting the arrest of a man named Corrigan, who had a playful bahit of picking up diamond rings from the dusts of the road, and disposing of the same to the unwary for a song. Sherrif Cole made the arrest down in Old Town. Knowing that he had corralled a man of infinite jest he concluded to find out something further than he know of the man with Shakespearean talents. So the Index advertised to the world that those who had been caught in the net set, and so agreeable worked, by Mr. Corrigan, the gentleman of Italian extraction, could call at the Sherriff’s office and upon presentation of the diamond rings, alleged, would receive back their money, as the official held nearly $409 of the fakir’s coin.

“They began to be heard from yesterday. They had seen the Index article and they wanted back their coin. The chance was open.”

“Now, there is no man on earth who likes to be picked up for a sucker. Perish such a thought! Anything and everything can go, buy the sucker act – oh no! Everybody is too cunning to be classed in that category.

“When George Miller called on Sheriff Cole, presented the ring with a sheepish expression swinging around his countenance, and wanted the $2 he had put up, a titter swung around the circle that would had made glad the risibilities [sic] of Mark Twain. Then came Henry Cole, a brother of the Sherriff, who had put up $2.50. And soon after Eli Curtis waltzed in, carrying a sheepish look, and wanted $5. Bob Poppett next made his appearance and wanted $2.40 – all the change he happened to have. And finally Matthew Cleghorn strolled leisurely into the grinding shop. He wanted $10. He had been touched for two of the beautiful stones. He thought he had struck a bonanza in the way of diamonds, and wasn’t going to let any get away if he could help it.

“Did any of the above gentlemen get their money back? Not any. Would any of them swear to a complaint? Nary one. Are any of them suckers? Not if they know themselves. They are too cute. They would not go before a magistrate and say they had been roped in by a messly old tramp or any one else.

“The result of the whole business is that so far no complaint has been filed against Mr. Corrigan for obtaining money under false pretenses, and the probability is that none will be unless the Sheriff comes to the front and relates what he knows about the affable Mr. Corrigan.”

17 Apr 1897 – WS 1 – RapBUS

“An Ideal Mountain Ranch, Nestling on the Side of Mount San Jacinto, A Trip Over the Mountain Road to A. B. McCrary’s Place – What it Produces,”

Idlewild, April 10., - (Special Correspondence of The Sun). – An invitation from my old time friend, Ab McCrary, to accompany him and party to his recently acquired ranch on San Jacinto mountain was readily accepted by me, as it would give me an opportunity of seeing something of farming in Southern California, at an altitude far removed from the home of the olive and the orange, and as I was so much charmed with all I saw I think it probable that an account of the trip might interest some of your readers. The time appointed for the start was 7:30 a.m. on Thursday, April 1, and at that hour I found three wagons loaded with young stock and a fourth, carrying camping outfit and provisions for the ranch, including certain medical comforts, to be used chiefly as an antidote for rattlesnake bites. We left Idlewild at the hour named, passed through Beaumont at 2 p.m. and camped at the foot of the mountain, on the ranch of Mr. Lawrence. Here the young calves were placed in the corral and horses, in the barn for the night.

Next morning four of the best horses were hitched to one wagon and the mountain climbing commenced, while two of the party, on foot, drove the stock before them. I rode beside E'. P. Lane, McCrary's uncle, who handled the ribbons, and his dexterity in driving over the stony road, up heavy grades and down steep declivities, excited both my wonder and astonishment till I learned that his occupation early in the ‘50s - "The days of old, the days of gold” - Had been for years, driving stage over the mountains of Utah and Montana, making quick time conveying the U. S. mails behind six spanking horses every day. He proved on this occasion that his hand had not yet "forgot its cunning."

Alter following the rough and stony road for four and a half miles, "Elephant rock" was reached. This consists of a monster boulder which, when the sun is in the right position, fairly represents the animal whose name it bears, but is of such mammoth proportions that had the late lamented Jumbo been placed by its side, he would have appeared but a pigmy in comparison.

Here, after working our way through the dreary wastes of underbrush, buck thorn and manzanita, with no animal life visible, except here and there a sleepy lizard basking on the warm side of a bare rock, the monotony of the scene was enlivened by the distant view of the green fields and pleasant homestead of the Poppett ranch down in the smiling valley beneath us, though it is situated at an elevation of 3,800 feet. It did not take us long to present ourselves at the hospitable door and receive hearty welcome. But time pressed so we passed through his green pastures, having to make something of a detour in conseque43nce of recent washouts, and pushed on up a rather steep grade, from the top of which we could look down on the pleasant valley, which comprises Ab McCrary’s mountain ranch. We estimated this as being somewhat higher up than the Poppett place, so call it 4,000 feet above sea level.

Down grade again for a couple of miles brought us to the ranch house, where we were heartily welcomed by Van Chipman, who is taking care of the place for McCrary for the present.

Here was to be seen an ideal mountain ranch, acres and acres of level meadow land, hedged on all sides by stately live oaks, the ground beneath which is literally covered with acorns, reminding me forcibly of “the husks that the swine did eat.” But what a place it would be to raise hogs! And only five miles from the railroad, and down grade all the way for the stock to travel. And then the water! So different from what we use in cities; cool, sparkling and bubbling from the fountainhead amongst the rocks, and so plentiful that it supplies the numerous brooks that meander through the plains, supplying the meadow grass with moisture and furnishing perpetual water for the stock, no matter how dry the season; then finding its way to the limits of the ranch and going to waster as it tumbles itself in miniature cascades amongst the rocks and crannies, soon losing itself amongst the sand and gravel in the numerous cafions.

What a pity to see so much waste, while in Banning, only five miles distant, they are being almost ruined for want of a water system such as could be supplied so close at home.

I could not help noticing the absence of anything in the shape of a washout, but found that all the storm water from the mountain is carried off into the cafions before it reaches the pleasant meadows of the ranch, and the torrents spend thie4r fury on the rugged rocks which act a natural harrier and protect the ranch from inundation.

Next day we proceeded to regular ranch work, and after branding the young stock "J-P," conjoined, we proceeded to the house and "branded" every mother's son on the place.

"Hold on," says McCrary, who had been looking over my shoulder as I write; ' hold on there! I want the straight truth, so leave that out about branding the men."

But I reminded him that when he gave water to his horses, he told me he had "watered" them, and I claimed the same right to say when I had given brandy to the men that I had "brandied" them, and I still maintain the right.

The neglected peach and apple orchards show the possibilities in that direction, and I am informed that exceptionally fine melons, pumpkins, potatoes and other vegetables have been raised here, and altogether, in the hands of such a man as its present owner, it is safe to predict that in two or three years from now he will have a very beautiful mountain home.

All along the hills surrounding the flat land are evidences of prospecting camps, and at the mouths of the various drives much metal-hearing rock is scattered around, but of what character or value my inexperience in such matters prevents me from expressing an opinion, but specimens were brought to the city for expert examination.

George, the former owner of the ranch, accompanied us on this trip and rounded up a few of his horses, which had remained here all winter, and found them in good condition, although at times there were 22 inches of snow on the ground. The object of our visit being accomplished we made an early start next day and retraced our steps over the mountain road, arriving at Idlewild at 6 p. m. same day, after a most successful and pleasant outing with some excellent photographs of the most interesting incidents of the journey.

Fredk. H. Rogers

25 Mar 1900 – SBS 7 – RapPIONEER

“PIONEER SOCIETY MEETING, Interesting Exercises and Speeches With a Number of the Old Guard Present,” –

Shortly after 2 p. m., yesterday, President Roberds called the attendance to order, and the minutes of the previous meeting were read and approved.

There were no sick reports and Pioneer Potter was present to speak for himself.

After the singing of the opening ode, Pioneer S. C. Cox proposed Robert Poppett for membership in the Society, and when the committee of investigation had acted, he was admitted by rising vote, and warmly congratulated upon his welcome advent to closer association with the organized '49ers.

Humorous clippings handed in by Sisters Earp, Yager, and Turley, were read by Assistant Secretary Stephen, who also renewed his motion in regard to the preservation of the giant sequoias. This was seconded by First Vice-President Holcomb, once a resident among them. After eloquent remarks from Pioneers Boren, Amos. Woodward, Brown and others the motion was unanimously carried, and much gratification expressed at the intention of Mrs. Hearst to donate liberally towards the expense of said preservation.

The Honorable Board of Supervisors was heartily commended for the steps being taken in the direction of providing a free road to the mountain summit, Pioneers Thomas, Brown and Stephen, especially expressing the gratefulness of the society for the long-desired movement.

Judge Knox, who fought alongside Lincoln and other notables in the Blackhawk war, said that he loved the men and women of '49, because they were of the Andrew Jackson type loyal to the people and their sacred Union of Republican States. His first vote was cast for the hero of New Orleans at Galena, Ill.

Col. Kelting. after long absence, was glad to lie again among his Argonautic associates, and remarked that be was failing physically, but would come to the meetings as often as possible.

Pioneer Bradford said that nothing cheered him up so much as a visit to Pioneer Hall, and hoped that his health would permit many more such exhilrants.

Pioneer Holcomb desired a large representation at the Episcopal church on the date selected. April 22, for the Rev. Brown's discourse to the Pioneers.

Prior to adjournment, Pioneer T. J. Wilson exhibited a rare specimen of cactus with a star-shaped bloom, which was symbolical of the Order of the Eastern Star. The cactus was referred to the floral committee for botanical diagnosis.

5 Dec 1900 – SBS 4 – RapBUS

“Colonizing Scheme for Old Mexico,” –

There was a large gathering of a hundred or more people yesterday afternoon at the court room of Justice Thomas, that at first sight looked to be a meeting of the Socialist party but a few minutes listening through the wide open door showed that it was an Imperialistic-Expansion scheme and that the objective point was across the line in the sister republic of Mexico. Socialist N. A. Richardson appeared to be at the head of the move, with Democratic Deputy Sheriff W. C. Seccombe as right hand supporter and Republican C. B. Morris on his left, while an enthusiastic applauder of the remarks was Prohibitionist Williams. T. J. Wilson was on the platform, evidently to assist in furnishing the rolling stock for the move, and lawyers, mechanics, merchants, and business men of all classics were present, all eager to assist in the great work of colonizing and annexing the State of Sonora from Mexican rule, to that of the United States.

Sonora is said to be the richest and best farming land in the whole country across the line, while the climate is such that it puts even Southern California in the shade being especially noticeable for the great abundance of water. There is a tract of land there of 280,000 acres that can be obtained for a song and each one of the 100 men yesterday afternoon was tuning his pipes to assist in singing that song and ten times as many more citizens would like to help but they are to be kept in the dark until the first hundred have secured the land.

The meeting yesterday was the first one that has been held by the signers or stock holders in the plan. It is stated that tin tract of 280.000 acres can be bought for $20,000. One hundred men have been "let in" so that for $200 they become the owners of 2,800 acres when the lands are divided.

The land belongs to an estate, the heirs of which are said to reside in Arizona, and it is proposed first to buy in $15 each, making a fund of $1500, and with this a committee is to be sent out to prospect and report. It was for the purpose of electing this committee that the meeting was held yesterday afternoon. Policeman T. C. Worden is first to go into Arizona to make arrangements for a meeting with the owners of the land, and then the committee of five, which was elected yesterday will go to inspect the land and complete the deal. Among the members of the committee elected yesterday were H. M. Barton and Robert Poppett.

For several years the Pioneer Society has championed this free mountain road proposition. The benefits to be derived therefrom are numerous. The poor men will now be enabled to procure firewood that is now decaying and endangering the forests; the hard worked and poorly paid teamsters will now be able to haul the lumber from our mountain mills and earn a fair living for their families and teams; the tourists will be attracted in summer to our mountains in increased numbers from all parts of California. "What was his name? I do not know his name;

I only know he heard God's voice and came;

Brought all he loved across the sea,

To live and work for God and me;

Felled the ungracious oak,

Dragged from the soil,

With torrid toll,

Thrice-gnarled roots and stubborn rock,

With plenty piled the haggard mountain side,

And at the end, without memorial, died;

No blaring trumpet sounded out his fame;

He lived, he died; I do not know his name.

No form of bronze and no memorial stones Show me the place where lie his mouldering bones;

Only a cheerful city stands,

Built by his hardened hands;

Only ten thousand homes

Where every day

The cheerful play

Of love and hope and courage comes.

These are his monuments, and these alone;

There is no form of bronze and no memorial stone."

Brother Jerry McElvaine was born In Kalamazoo, Michigan, November 21, 1829; at 11 years of age his father, Jeremiah, Sr.. died, from that on he had to earn his own living. In 1859 he married Miss Minerva Morse; engaged In teaming and freighting to Salt Lake and as far as Montana. In later years he has lived a quiet, simple life, beloved by all his neighbors and friends who will miss his genial face as. he was so well known In this community. His time seemed to come when enjoying the sweet comforts of home in the midst of his family, without pain, without sorrows, without sickness, without anxiety, perfectly resigned to the will of his Heavenly Father. “Former Utah Man is Amazed at Prosperity, Robert Poppett Tells Neighbors at San Bernardino Something About Growth of Beehive State,” –

Robert Poppett, one of the oldest residents of San Bernardino valley, having come out to this section from Utah in 1854, returned this week from a visit to his boyhood haunts in Utah. Yesterday Mr. Poppett paid the Free Press a pleasant visit and told something of his trip, says the San Bernardino (Cal.) Free Press.

He, in company with his daughter, Miss Leah Poppett, and Mrs. Allie Benson, spent two months in the Mormon state. They toured the state from Springville to Ogden, stopping at the former city, Park City, Provo, Pleasant Grove, Fountain Green and Salt Lake. They spent two weeks in the capital city and visited all points of interest there, including the famous tabernacle. Mr. Poppett says there is enough water wasted annually in Salt Lake to irrigate a large portion of the San Bernardino valley.

He is very enthusiastic over the outlook, present conditions and fertility of Utah lands, and reports splendid crops of berries and fruits of all natures, save citrus fruits.

He says the lands that could be bought when he was a boy at $1 an acre are selling for $200 to $800 now, and that raw land in Utah is as high as raw land here.

He reports a pleasant visit among his old friends and says the prosperity and richness of Utah was a revelation to him.

“The Pioneer,” – The pioneers seem to live the sentiment of the poet when he says “When but few years of life remain, ‘tis life renewed to laugh them o’er again”.

For they feel inspired by the retrospect of a long and useful life, a retrospect that brings to them hope and satisfaction and a resignation to the will of Him who doeth all things well. The meeting of yesterday In Native Sons' hall proved no exception to the former gatherings for smiling countenances and buoyancy of spirits. President George Miller was never in a happier mood and called Sister Susan Heath and Brother Printz as supporters, thanked Sisters Alexander and Crandall for beautiful bouquets and congratulated the members on the lively and interesting reunions of lifelong friends, more appreciated by those whose ties of friendship have been cemented for over half a century and the many vicissitudes of life have made stronger and stronger.

After roll call and reading of the minutes. Sister Stuchberry was reported still confined to her home, but hopeful for a speedy recovery from her protracted illness. Brother W. S. Tittle has improved so that he was able to attend to business. Sisters Case, Crandall and Yager submitted a glowing report of the golden wedding celebrated at last meeting in which our venerable pioneers, Brother and Sister W. F. Holcomb were the chief participant, as bride and groom of 50 years ago, being in faith health, surrounded by a happy family of sons and daughters, and a large circle of grandchildren to cheer Grandma and Grandpa on their way to the diamond wedding day, a fond wish also entertained for them by their hundreds of friends all over Southern California, and especially by all their admiring neighbors by whom they have lived for many years.

These golden weddings are quite an attraction among the Pioneers. Brother and Sister R. T. Roberds celebrated theirs not long ago. Brother and Sister McElvain commemorated theirs early in the: year and soon thereafter Brother Jerry McElvain was called away to his heavenly home.

President Miller noticing: Messrs. L. Espelin, George H. Romrell and D. R. Taylor in the audience, extended a cordial welcome to them and invited them to address the society. Each responded with a message of good cheer and appreciation of the noble work of the Pioneers, indulging in the hope that the last days of the Pioneers may be the most delightful and implored our Heavenly Father to grant these blessings.

Past President Woodward by request reviewed the history of the Argonauts, classed them with empire builders, referred to the superhuman courage required to leave happy homes to venture into an unknown wilderness with dangers on every hand to establish on Pacific's shore a civilization that is now the admiration of the United States and the whole world. The proudest monuments of the pioneers he claimed to be the Native Daughters and the Native Sons of the Golden West.

Past President R. T. Roberds was prevailed on to recount some of his experiences in crossing the plains, and after some persuasion favored the society with of the most comprehensive recitals of pioneering that he ever ventured to give to his sisters and brothers. He began in the Rocky mountains near Fort Pueblo, where Secretary Brown was born, where he became acquainted with the trappers and mountaineers James W. Waters, John Brown. Sr., V. J. Herring, Kit Carson and others then started west, crossing the dangerous Green river, stopping at Salt Lake, then on past the Humboldt, Carsonn valley, Hangtown and Sutter's Fort, coming all the way with ox team suffering at times for provisions, living on hominy and milk from the faithful cows that were frequently worked by the side of the oxen. For three days he lived on wild rose berries, all of which if written just as it happened would make an interesting volume for the present and future generations to read and learn to appreciate the true worth and heroism of those who with the rifle and the axe cleared the way for western civilization.

Past President W. F. Holcomb by earnest solicitation, favored the society with some of his experiences in crossing the plains in 1850. He lost all his provisions and clothing in trying to cross Green river, walked most of the way barefooted across the American continent to Hangtown where he began mining with varied experiences. President Miller responded briefly, the meeting taking the form of an experience revival, proving of unusual interest. He was followed by Brother Robert Poppett with pleasant remarks.

Sisters Louisa Waters and Susanna Rathbun contributed cheerful and encouraging thoughts and the members were delightful to see them present and participate in the joyous occasion.

San Bernardino, Calif., July 13th, 1912.

To President George Miller, and to the members of the Pioneer Society:

Dear Sisters and Brothers: I regret very much, more than I am able to express to you by inability to be with you at this, the last meeting before our summer vacation. I had planned to make a brief report to you of my trip to Albuquerque, New Mexico, near Santa Fe, Taos, Pueblo, Colorado, the country of the trappers and hunters of the Rocky Mountains: Kit Carson, Fremont, Williams, Waters, Herring, and you will pardon me for naming Brown among them. Brave souls who braved the dangers of the wilderness of mountains, plains and deserts to open the way for Western civilization.

I wished also to assure you how well your minutes and proceedings read to us at Albuquerque and at the Grand Canyon so faithfully kept by our good Sisters Waters and Wilson.

I wished also to report the most enjoyable time Mrs. Brown and I enjoyed at the home of our daughter, Mrs. C. H. Wiggett at Albuquerque, during our three weeks' visit with them and the celebration of our 36th wedding anniversary, July 4th, at a family re-union.

Then our visit of four days at the Grand Canyon, that wonder of wonders, spell binding and awe-inspiring work of our Heavenly Father, which we visited on our way home, and only wished that you all could stand by our side and enjoy this grand, magnificent, sublime scene.

I am denied the pleasure of communing with you this day in our dear little Log Cabin, by two urgent telegrams calling me to the desert at Palm Springs on important professional business. I have the books all prepared to date and will notify our dear Sister Rose Crandall Wilson, so she will serve you at this meeting along with Sister Waters if her home duties permit her to attend.

So have a good meeting and, in closing it, I recommend that the best wishes of the Society be extended to all the sick and to all the Sisters and Brothers that they may all enjoy a happy vacation in the mountains, at the sea shore, or at home, wherever they may desire, and that we may all meet again September 7th, strengthened and ready to go on with the good work of the Pioneers on Admission Day, is the wish of your old secretary.

JOHN BROWN, Jr.

A New Legend - The following new legend of the Arrowhead was read, and created much interest and amusement:

Many, many years ago the Serrano indians, a branch of the Coahuilla Mission Indians of Southern California, established a large rancheria or village on the flat where the hot springs are, a little north and east of the present site of the famous Arrowhead hotel, at the base of what is now known as Arrowhead mountain, attracted to this picturesque retreat by the curative properties of the hot water issuing from the springs and the canyon near by on the west; and also by the abundance of game on all sides, which afforded them food. Here they lived in ease and contentment, in the midst of the inspiring mountains surrounding them, and imagined they saw the Great Spirit in the clouds, and heard him in the wind and thunder.

It happened then, as it has happened since even among civilized communities, that two of these braves fell in love with and sought the hand of a dusky maiden, Maria, daughter of the chief, Gabriel, and so earnestly did they press their devotions that Gabriel was obliged to take these wooings in hand himself, and ordered them settled at once according to the ancient custom of his tribe, the ordeal of mortal combat with bows and arrows, the conqueror to be entitled to his dearly purchased bride. This chieftain fixed the day, summoned all his tribe to attend and see fair play. The solemn day arrived; 40 paces were stepped off by him, the antagonists, Santos and Angelo, were ordered to face one another, the bows were carefully examined and found in excellent condition, the arrows being provided with the hardest and sharpest arrowheads of flint rock that could be found in the mountains; and when all was in readiness, Chief Gabriel having his daughter by his side, gave the command to fire, and the duel was on, but did not last long. But few arrows left their quiver when a well directed arrow from the bow of Angelo pierced the heart of Santos who fell to the ground exclaiming "Maria is yours." Angelo approached his victim, pulled the bleeding arrow from his dying rival, placed it in his bow and with all his strength, and in the moment of his triumph shot this arrow with its bleeding arrowhead away up on the mountain side where it was imbedded in the earth, and gradually began to grow and finally attained its present size, the noted land mark of the San Bernardino valley, the Arrowhead, that mysterious configuration that attracts the attention and excites the wonder of the thousands who live in and pass through the lovliest valley in all California.

It is needless to say that Chief Gabriel ordered a great feast, and called his tribe together and midst dancing and rejoicing, gave to Angelo his daughter Maria in marriage. The bride and groom lived a long and happy life, whose descendants still live at San Manuel, on the mountain side north of the Southern California State hospital, who treasure this romantic legend and confided it to their old time friend, John Brown Jr., of this city.

 “Resolved, by the San Bernardino Society of California Pioneers, that the name of the old city cemetery be changed to Pioneer cemetery, and we recommend our Honorable Mayor, George H. The pioneers, with their children, grand-children and great grand-children,native sons and daughters, and many friends, enjoyed an all day basket picnic at Urbita Springs, that popular resort of the Southern California Citrus Belt, yesterday, and received many courtesies from Mr. Somer, superintendent of the springs.

This discussion branched off as to how many present drove oxen across the plains, and walked most of the way, and had to travel in bare feet. Sisters Hadden and Devening gave interesting reports of their visits to the San Francisco international exposition. Mrs. Krouse, of New York, has become an enthusiastic booster for San Bernardino climate and the grand and picturesque surroundings. JOHN BROWN, JR.

“GIVES LAND TO CHILDREN FOR CHRISTMAS, Robert Poppett, Pioneer, Hands Eight Sons and Two Daughters Deeds,” –

After 51 years ownership [1864], Robert Poppett, widely known pioneer, yesterday divided his valuable five-acre tract of land between D and E and Thirteenth and Fourteenth streets, and presented the deeds to his 10 children as Christmas presents.

For years Mr. Poppett had planned the division of the property, and for that reason he had declined repeated offers for the land, which is in the heart of the residence district.

The D street frontage goes to John, Ed, July and Thurman Poppett. The E street frontage goes to George, Moroson, Grover, Ira, Mrs. Irene Poppett Hitchcock and Miss Leah Poppett. With the exception of George Poppett, a resident of Nevada, all the Poppett family live in San Bernardino.

The home place is given to Mrs. Hitchcock.

The frontage of the D street lots is 70 feet. On E street it is 59 feet. The lots are all 300 feet deep.

It was 65 years ago that Mr. Poppett came to San Bernardino. He was then a boy of 11 years. He had come from England as a lad, with a missionary. Other property he owned adjoining the tract he has now given to his children he sold several years ago.

7 May 1916 – SBS – RapPIONEER

“Pioneers,” – Disinterested visitors at the log cabin in Pioneer park were signally favored yesterday in being able to participate in a gathering that in all probability will never be duplicated in this city. It was the ninety-fourth birthday of Uncle Joseph Hancock, and his family connections came to do him honor.

Chief among these was Aunt Nancy Daley, widow of the late Edward Daley, and daughter of the late Captain Hunt, and the mother of some of the most prominent residents of this valley.

Sister Daley presented a motion of honor and respect for the guest of the day, and stated that she had known him for 82 years [1834] , ever since he came to her father's house when she was 7 years old, one evening, shouting, "Wolf!" "Wolf!" "Come out with your gun and shoot the wolf!" She said that in all the 82 years of acquaintanceship and friendship she had known Uncle Joseph as the soul of honor.

The meeting was called to order by President Boren at 2 o'clock. Sister Crouse at the piano and Brother Morse with the violin accompanied the opening ode, which was sung with fervor, all present participating. An invocation was offered by Chaplain Shaw. Uncle Joseph Hancock and Aunt Nancy Daley were the right and left hand supporters. The president thanked all those who had decorated the tables with beautiful flowers Sisters Alexander, Harrison, Nora Miller, J. O. Lamb, Girard, Case, Hamilton and Crouse. There were roses, baby roses and roses grown up, and of many varieties, sweet William, carnations, geraniums, hollyhocks, Shasta daisies and greenery.

Reports from the sick showed all under the weather greatly improved. Sister Jane Smithson reported for the committee on May day, a glorious celebration, and a signal success. The queen of May, Sister Harrison, the bishop, Brother Robert Poppett, and many others made very pleasant remarks. The treasurer, Sister Alexander, reported all bills paid and a handsome balance in the treasury. Russell Collins, a poly high student, reported for about 50 other young people of the city, that grandchildren of pioneers had organized a society called "The Young Pioneer Club," to operate under the rules and regulations of the Pioneer society, with the main object of furnishing additions to the program of the Saturday afternoon Pioneer meetings. By vote, presented by Father Hancock and seconded by Mother Daley, the young people were heartily thanked for their kind plans for the society.

Under "good of the order," the president called on Secretary John Brown, Jr., to engineer the program of the day. The following relatives of Uncle Joseph Hancock were present and took part:

  • Sister Nora Miller, a daughter,

  • Sister Jerusha Tyler, a daughter,

  • Sister Molly Tyler Bright, a granddaughter, and her mother.

  • Sister J. S. Bright;

It was reported that Miss Lois Erma Bausch, 2 days old, was great great grand daughter of Uncle Joseph Hancock, and that the little miss had four happy grandmothers and four proud grandfathers. In this connection, Sister Kirkpatrick rose to say that she was the great grandmother of a boy who has seven grandmothers and two grandfathers. Other great, great grandmothers, grandfathers and grandmothers would have reported, but time was limited.

At this point the master of ceremonies spoke of Uncle Joseph Hancock, stating that his grandfather was Henry Hancock, who was a soldier of the revolutionary war, and a brother of John Hancock, chairman of the declaration of Independence committee, and one of the signers of that famous document. John Hancock was a shoemaker, and his hammer is now in the possession of the Pioneer society, among its treasures. Sister Nora Miller then stated that she would like to honor her mother's memory. Her name was Nancy A. Hancock and she then unrolled a package in which was carefully preserved a pair of "cards" her mother had used, and proceeded to "card" some cotton, first making a "bat" to be used as stuffing for a quilt, and then a "roll" to be spun into thread, all of which was of exceeding interest to the entire company. The guest of honor made a few remarks, expressing his delight at being present and stating that his present occupation was the very pleasant one of driving about the valley with his own horse and rig, calling on old acquaintances, and enjoying the outdoors. He stated that he had seen seven generations of his own blood relatives, having called on the latest baby, and having seen his honored grandfather.

Fitting resolutions were heartily passed extending to Father Hancock and also to Mother Daley the love and respect of the society, and congratulating them on their remarkable preservation. Sister Hadden and Jane Smithson, and Brother Poppett, and many others spoke to these resolutions.

At this point. Sister Hadden was asked to take up the duties of master of ceremonies. Brother George Miller held the meeting for half an hour telling of a trip he took during his boyhood days. He had for companions Paul Van Curen, a man by the name of Landers, and William Rhodes. They were always guarding against Indians and over in Death Valley district while following the wagon, ox, and horse tracks of the unfortunate settlers who went there and perished they came upon three Indians. All around there were pieces of iron off the wagons which had been burned by the Indians. Soon about 35 more came to rescue their friends, but as they had no guns there was no trouble. Taking one as a guide, they went to the further end of the valley where, although no vegetation was visible, they found a water hole in the ridges also a large number of Piutes who it seems were hunting ducks. Brother Miller described their manner of cooking and eating. They seemed to subsist on tullies, ducks, and lizards. A gambling game was in progress and one prosperous buck lost all the clothes he possessed. The object of the trip was to find the money that was buried by the settlers before they tiled and a very rich mine that was reported to be in that territory. Neither was found and the party soon returned to San Bernardino.

Sister Crouse called attention to the fact that today was St. Patrick’s day and that St. Patrick was a pioneer in his country.

Lawrence B. Gannon of Chicago was a very interested visitor.

Sister Case and Brother Morse entertained with music after the closing of the meeting.

The pioneers well remember April 26, 1853, for on that day the legislature of California passed the act creating the county of San Bernardino from the eastern portion of Los Angeles county. By that act section five, Colonel Isaac Williams, David Seely, John Brown, and H. G. Sherwood were appointed and constituted a board of commissioners to designate the election precincts in the new county of San Bernardino; for the election of the officers at the first election; to appoint the inspectors of election; to receive the returns of election and to issue certificates of election.

In accordance with such act, an election was held in January 1854, and certificates of election were issued by this board to D. M. Thomas, county judge; Robert Ciift, sheriff; R. R. Some History Given."In 1842 Don Pablo Belarde, who came over this trail with a colony from New Mexico, is alive at this date, December 1917, and will attend the dedication.

"In 1844 James W. Waters used this trail in coming to San Diego, California, with a pack train loaded with Navajo blankets and took back with him abalone shells to trade with the Rocky Mountain Indians.

"In 1847 Capt. Jefferson Hunt came over this trail with a-party to get stock to drive to Utah, and in 1849 piloted the first immigrant train over this trail to California.

"In 1849 Sheldon Stoddard passed over this trail with pack mules on his way to the mines near Sacramento, California, with Gen. Charles C. Rich, George Q. Cannon, William Flake, David Seely and eleven others. At the Muddy, met Sydney P. Waite on his way to California with Captain Hunt's train.

"In 1849 Sheldon Stoddard saw James W. Waters, Joseph Bridger and about four others at Hobble creek, now known as Springfield, Utah, on their way over this trail, to the mines at Mariposa, California.

"In 1849 the wagon train known on the desert as the Pomeroy train having large Missouri wagons, was unable to come down through Coyote pass, so went west on the summit, and came down the Hog Back into Cajon pass end passed on down the canyon to Los Angeles.

"In. the spring of 1851, Capt. Jefferson Hunt, Amasa Lyman, Gen. Charles C. Rich, David Seely, Andrew Lytle as guides and wagonmasters, with about 300 pioneers passed down this trail to San Bernardino, stopping about six months at Sycamore Grove, now the Glen Helen Ranch, until the San Bernardino Rancho was bought from the Lugo family, the first intention being to purchase the Chino Rancho from Col. Isaac Williams. March 25, 1853, San Bernardino county was organized.

"January 21, 1888, the San Bernardino Society of California Pioneers was organized, George Lord, first president."

Monument Address.

Friends, and Dear Pioneers: - It is indeed a blessed privilege to be allowed the honor of accepting, in behalf of the Native Daughters of the Golden West, this gift, a share in this beautiful monument, that stands at the gateway, and marks the path of our beloved parents, into this lovely valley. And though words seem inadequate, I want to express the appreciation of the Native Daughters for this beautiful gift, and also for the many wonderful things that have come to us through your heroic efforts.

Sixty-eight years ago today, the dear souls that had the brains and brawn to leave their homes and journey across almost unknown wilds, with danger linking at every turn, arrived at this spot and, looking, saw that great valley just beyond. Keeping straight ahead, with their covered wagons and a few "gees" and "haws" to trusty oxen, and probably hub deep in mud, but with light hearts, they arrived in San Bernardino, just a few days before Christmas. What a wonderful Christmas it must have been for those men and women. But times have changed indeed. I think you builded better than you knew.

Today we left the city at 9 o'clock, in conveyances propelled by their own motive power, over the same road, but smoothly paved, and arrived here in just 45 minutes. Ask Grandfather Stoddard, or any of the boys, and they will assure you that thy did not make that time in ’49 [1849].

It seems like a dream, does it not. But it must be a very beautiful dream to the men and women who have lived during those interesting years.

A friend of mine said to me, “Letty, all of the old pioneers seem like brothers and sisters.” Well, they are. In spirit they have always lived in one great brotherhood. They lived during the times that tried men’s souls, never thinking of self, but the protection of all. Social rank was cast aside and every man was his brother’s keeper. And they learned, through hard knocks, that wonderful lesson of brotherly love and self-denial. Times of great stress surely make us all of one kin.

Now we are in the midst of this terrible turmoil, for this is indeed the world’s stupendous hour, and I hope that if we, your sons and daughters, are called upon to do seemingly hard tasks, that we may have the courage and will-power of our parents to say, yes, gladly and do it with a smile. If we are called upon to give up this, that or the other thing, that seems so essential to us, let us close our eyes and see, many years ago, the covered wagons crawling across the great expanse of desert and those men and women trudging most of that weary distance and sometimes with no covering for their feet and sometimes with scarcely food enough to keep body and soul together, many of them giving up their lives. Then today picture our boys in uniform, giving their young lives to save our precious freedom and we will not mind giving up anything or everything that the world may enjoy the same great and blessed democracy that has been our heritage.

We, your children, are glad to be able to meet you here today and in the name of the present generation in the name of Liberty to thank you.

JOHN BROWN, Secretary. REETTA V. HADDEN Assistant Secretary.

SBS 5 –

“Robert Poppett, Prominent as Pioneer, Passes on Last Eve; Enoch Wiltfong, Pioneer, Also,” –

Robert Poppett, after many other remedies have failed, died last night at his home in 1431 D street, closing a life, the incidents of which assembled would read like fiction. He was born at Shropshire, April 28, 1839, one of the early pioneers of the San Bernardino valley when it was in the rough.

He was 78 years of age and a native of England, but came to America when but 10 years of age. With the adventurers of the early fifties, he drifted westward and after a thrilling trip from Utah he settled in this valley in 1854, making it his home since that time.

For the past few years, Mr. Poppett had been in ill health and some time ago underwent an operation in the effort to better his health. He apparently was recovering when he suffered a relapse and his death last night not entirely unexpected, but the cause of sorrow among the hundreds of people who had known him for many years and among the members of a large family.

He made his home at 1431 D street for many years following his retirement from a life of activity and usefulness. Mr. Poppett was a familiar figure among the citizens of the city years ago and of late years was one of the most conspicuous of the Pioneer society members.

He came to San Bernardino in 1854 and like many other of the young men of that period, he became a freight and stage team driver across the desert, making long trips to desert points. He later became a rancher.

In 1863 he married Miss Alice Case of this city and to them were born 12 children, 10 of whom are living at the present. They are John R., G. W., Edward, Huley, Thurman, Morrison, F., Ira and Grover Poppett. Mrs. Irene Hitchcock and Miss Leah Poppett.

Robert Poppett was a freighter between San Bernardino and silver mines at Ivanpah and Arizona communities, operating a 12-mule team.

His mother died when he was three years old and he came from England to the United States by boat with another family.

He arrived at Council Bluffs, Iowa, in 1842, and with still another family went to Salt Lake City, Utah, in 1849. Robert Poppett lived with various families in Salt Lake until he came to San Bernardino in 1854.

After 28 years, Mr. Poppett received the first news of his father through a Salt Lake newspaper. He sent his father money to come to the United States, the latter spending his last 15 years of life here.

Robert Poppet's mules were kept at the southeast corner of Tenth and E streets. The original family home was in the Fourteen-hundred block of E street, where Irene Poppett Hitchcock now lives.

The family is widely-known throughout the valley. Ed Poppett is a member of the San Bernardino police force, having served longer on the force than any other man. He is now in his thirty-third year as a police officer.
